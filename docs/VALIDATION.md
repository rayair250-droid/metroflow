# MetroFlow — model validation and statistical rigor

This note records the checks used to argue the model behaves correctly, not
merely that it runs. All numbers below are reproducible from the committed code
(`metroflow/validation.py`, `metroflow/experiment.py`) and are re-generated by
`python examples/generate_examples.py`. They are illustrative of the model, not
of any real network.

## 1. Little's Law (conservation check)

For a stable queueing system in steady state, the time-average number in the
system equals the arrival rate times the mean time in system: **L = λ·W**. We
run a stationary, lightly-loaded, incident-free line (`stable_config()`: flat
demand, generous frequency and capacity, ~0 denied boardings) and measure L, λ
and W over the *same* post-warm-up window so the identity is tested at steady
state.

Result (`baseline`, seed 42):

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| L (mean total passengers waiting) | 13.9 |
| λ (boarding throughput, pax/s)    | 0.1217 |
| W (mean wait, s)                  | 127.9 |
| λ·W (predicted L)                 | 15.6 |
| relative error                    | **10.8%** |
| denial rate                       | 0.0000 |

Agreement is within ~11% (and <25% across seeds 1/7/99), which is the expected
tolerance for a finite discrete-event run. The check is only meaningful because
the denial rate is essentially zero — Little's Law would not hold if a
non-negligible fraction of arrivals never boarded (their time in system is
unbounded). Test: `tests/test_validation.py::test_littles_law_holds_in_steady_state`.

## 2. Bunching reproduction and suppression

Train (and bus) **bunching** is a well-documented instability: load-dependent
dwell is a positive feedback — a slightly late train meets a larger crowd,
dwells longer, falls further behind, and the train behind it closes the gap.
Starting from an almost evenly-spaced fleet, headway variability should grow
over the run.

We pool one direction's headways into the first and last third of the horizon
and compare their coefficient of variation (`bunching_config()`: flat demand so
the only driver is the instability).

| Run | early-window headway CV | late-window headway CV |
|---|---|---|
| no control          | 0.49 | **1.63** (3.3× growth) |
| even-headway holding | 0.24 | **0.65** (suppressed) |

Without control the perturbation amplifies 3.3× (grows in 8/8 seeds tested). The
classic real-world countermeasure — **forward-headway holding** (hold a train
that would depart too soon after its leader, to restore the target headway) —
brings late-run variability back down toward the regular start. Reserve-train
*injection*, by contrast, targets crowding (denied boardings, wait, EJT) rather
than headway evenness, and does not by itself regularise headways; this is why
the suppression demonstration uses holding. Tests:
`test_bunching_grows_without_control`, `test_holding_control_suppresses_bunching`.

## 3. Sensitivity analysis

`sensitivity_sweep()` sweeps one parameter and records an outcome metric at a
fixed seed. Sweeping arrival intensity (`demand.arrival_scale`) produces a
monotonically increasing denied-boarding curve, as expected — more demand into a
fixed fleet means more pass-ups. Test:
`test_sensitivity_sweep_monotone_in_demand`.

## 4. Monte-Carlo comparison with confidence intervals

A single seed is an anecdote. `metroflow experiment` runs N seeded replications
per controller (common random numbers: replication *i* faces the identical
demand and incident realisation for every controller) and reports each metric's
mean with a 95% t-based confidence interval, plus a Welch's t-test of the
primary metric against the baseline.

`rush_incident`, 20 replications, base seed 42 — denied boardings:

| Controller | mean ± 95% CI | Welch p vs baseline | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| baseline   | 9003 ± 1147 | — | — |
| reactive   | 5542 ± 1269 | 0.00014 | **significant** (CIs disjoint) |
| predictive | 5733 ± 1263 | 0.00028 | **significant** (CIs disjoint) |
| optimizer  | 6636 ± 1187 | 0.0047  | **significant** (CIs disjoint) |

All three controllers reduce denied boardings by a statistically significant
margin (p < 0.005, and the confidence intervals do not overlap the baseline).
The differences *among* the controllers are smaller than their confidence
intervals here, so on this scenario they are not separable at 20 replications —
which the report states rather than over-claiming.
